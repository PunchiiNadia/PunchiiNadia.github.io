State bird

In 1931, Idaho's state lawmakers decided that the mountain bluebird would represent their state. These birds are around 7 inches in size, sporting a striking azure blue color with a white underbelly. The males have a blue vest as well. Female bluebirds have a more subdued blue-gray appearance. They typically lay about six or seven eggs that are blue-white in color. Bluebirds usually make their nests in hollow trees or crevices. They're pretty tidy creatures, always making sure to keep their nests clean by carrying any waste far away from it.

State flower

The syringa was designated the state flower of Idaho by the legislature in 1931. It is a branching shrub up to 12 feet tall with clusters of white, fragrant flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer. The blossoms are similar to the mock orange, have four petals, and the flowers grow at the ends of short, leafy branches.

State horse

Appaloosa has been designated to be the official state horse in 1975. Most of them are dominent in colorful spotted coat patterns, striped hooves, mottled skin, and white sclera around the eye. Opinions vary on how spotted horses first arrived in America.

State flag

Idaho's state flag was chosen by the state legislature in 1927. The emblem on the flag was created by Emma Edwards Green in 1863, making her the only woman to design a state seal. The flag's origins date back to 1907 when the Idaho regiment was preparing to join the fight against Spain.

State tree

Western White Pine

Our state tree, the Western White Pine, mostly find in northern Idaho where the largest bunch of them is still hanging around. They are dominent in blue-green needles and slim cones and can grow up to a whopping 100 feet tall. They also be known as straight grain and soft, smooth texture.
